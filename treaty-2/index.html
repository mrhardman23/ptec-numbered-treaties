Treaty 2

What is a Treaty:

A treaty is a formal agreement between nations. The treaty establishes the responsibilities of each nation in their relationship with the other and is legally binding. In the case of Numbered Treaties 1 to 11 in Canada, they were between the Canadian government and the Indigenous peoples. The Numbered Treaties of Canada provided the framework for how Indigenous peoples and the Canadian government would live together and share the land.

There was a huge difference between how the Indigenous peoples saw the Treaties and how the government did. For the Indigenous peoples, treaty meant to bring things together and create sacred promises. They saw the treaty as a way to build relationships and share the land. This contrasts with the Canadian government who used words like surrendered or gave up. To the Canadian government, the treaties were a way to make the Indigenous peoples give up their land.

Treaty 2:

Treaty 2 was signed shortly after Treaty 1. Signed in August 1871 at the Manitboa House Fur Trading Post, the land of Treaty 2 extends North and West of Treaty 1 territory and was three times the size of Manitoba at the time. The treaty contains 21,440,909 acres of land or roughly 86,768 square kilometers.

The Canadian Government aimed to take the Indigenous peoples' land and assimilate them and their culture into European society. Indigenous people were concerned for their lands and traditional ways and wanted to secure their future by forming a good relationship with the Canadian government.

Who was Involved:

Treaty Two was signed by the Canadian Goverment and the Anishinaabe peoples.
